Strike through is for things disabled until we restore driving the motor ourselves instead of diddling its power.
The code deployed to the field has been tagged "deployed-tuesday".
The git repo still has AccelStepper.* in it but those are not used in the build. They are from the Arduino library of that name. They are better than the regular library as they do NOT block when moving which allows us to do things like check the home sensor and stop and clear the stepper coordinate value rather than do a worst case pounding on the mechanism at startup or puzzle reset.

diagnostic commands

(Extracted from loop()'s switch on characters read from serial)
'p' turn motor power on
'o' turn motor power off
' ' (space char) show sensor and motion state info
'.' (period) ask motor to stop moving
'/' act like sensor just became dry
',' act like sensor just became wet
'!' executes most of startup.
Manage configuration state:
'?' show configuration info (stuff that gets saved)
'\' (backslash) reset all to compiled in values (a bunch of assignment statements in class Puzzle)
'`' (back tick) Undoing temporary changes (reread EEProm)
'|' (vertical bar) Saving parameters (save into EEProm)
Tweakable parameters are selected by the following letters, w is the powerup default:
'w' wetness threshold WetnessRequired;
't' track length trackLength;
'h' wetness hysteresis WetnessHysteresis;
'a' acceleration Acceleration;
's' max speed MaxSpeed;
'r' wetness sensor sampling rate
'f' "in a row" filter parameter for wetness

Values are raised or lowered by the letters used for Roman numerals, lower case is subtracted, upper case added: (function romanize)
'i' 'v' 'x' 'l' 'c' 'd' 'm'
1 5 10 50 100 500 1000

For instance, to alter track length from 100 to 127:  "txxvii".
You don't need to send them in one Serial Monitor click, characters are inspected individually and space and newlines are ignored.
When you are happy with the config '|' saves it, and you can test it mostly with '!', but should test with a power cycle of the board before trusting it fully.

Reverse engineering

The internal control board has the motor connector,
a connector for an optical pickup for the home sensor,
a PWM output for the LED,
a UART connector,
4 stake pins connector that I either didn't figure out or forgot what it does,
and a connector for what is likely a MCU downloader/debugger.

I gather that to get the thing to do something more than its back and forth we need to talk to it via the serial port, then use the stake pins for triggers. Unfortunately the connector is metric spaced and I don't have the associated parts to make a nice cable.

Present state of the gear

A T-tap has been added to the flower's homed/closed sensor. A cable leads from there to a board that has a resistor divider and a zener to bring that level down to 3.3V logic.
The tap board has a stepper motor connector that we are not using at present. The cables can be left dangling with no concern other than their tips shorting out something else.

A relay is driven by the Arduino and interrupts the 12V power going to the flower. The default state is such that Arduino failing usually leaves power ON. Power will go on when downloading a program.

The firmware still has some of the parts in it for driving the motor, but the stepping has been bypassed and what was the track length in steps is now milliseconds to be on for the opening pass. Putting the motor stuff back in will require minimal editing of existing source. The blooming_flower sketch folder still has a stepper driver library in it, but that is not presently getting built.

The sketchbook is my personal but public git repo, laptop #3 presently has my credentials cached in it.
